About Me

Hi, I'm Jessy Wang, a second-year Ph.D. student in Emerging Media Studies at Boston University. My work focuses on the psychological determinants of human-computer interaction, leveraging computational, qualitative, and community participatory design methods to develop digital tools that address real-world mental health challenges. Decoding HPV vaccine discourse: A ChatGPT-driven health belief analysis on social media.

Wang, J., Lian, J., Xu, L., & Lokmanoglu, A. D. (2025).

Communication Methods and Measures. Manuscript under review.

Keywords: HPV vaccination, GPT-annotation, prompt engineering, time series analysis, text analysis, health communication, social media discourse

Abstract: Human Papillomavirus (HPV) infection remains a global public health concern, with persistently low vaccination rates contributing to significant health risks. Despite previous research on HPV vaccine perceptions, challenges in large-scale data analysis and achieving ideal accuracy persist. To address this gap, this study developed a GPT-Health Belief Model (HBM) program to efficiently annotate HPV-related discourse on Twitter. Through text labeling and time series analysis of 5,575 tweets, this study elucidates distinct patterns and dynamic relationships within public health beliefs, particularly in the context of vaccination. The application of large language models, including GPT, demonstrated high reliability, achieving Krippendorff's alpha scores between 0.87 and 0.96, which underscores the model's accuracy in classifying nuanced health communication data. Time series analysis revealed temporal relationships among the components of the Health Belief Model (HBM) and behavioral tendencies. These analyses highlight the potential of advanced statistical techniques to uncover the temporal dynamics that shape public discourse and attitudes toward health behaviors over time. This approach demonstrates the potential of GPT-based models to enhance understanding of health beliefs in social media discourse.

Don’t Browse It, Experience It: Spatial Presence Mediated the Effect of 3D Marketing Websites.

Yang, J., Wang, J., Wang, Y., & Lin, J. (2025).

Computers in Human Behavior. Manuscript under review.

Keywords: 3D websites, non-immersive virtual reality, spatial presence, purchase intention, consumer learning, e-commerce, v-commerce

Abstract: With the development of technologies, 3D websites, which display dynamic and interactive virtual environments on the screen, emerged as an innovative marketing tool. Despite previous research has explored the effectiveness of 3D websites on consumer behaviors, few studies have examined the potential mediation effect of spatial presence in such effectiveness. Aiming to fill this research gap, the current study conducted two pre-registered experiments. By comparing the effect of 3D websites to static 2D websites in a commercial (Experiment 1) and a non-commercial context (Experiment 2), we found that 3D websites could elicit users’ feelings of entertainment (i.e., enjoyment and appreciation) and a greater purchase intention through spatial presence. These results validate the value of 3D websites as a marketing tool and shed light on a possible underlying mechanism. The theoretical and practical implications are further discussed.

From Digital Group Game to Deeper Bonds: Enhancing Self-Disclosure Capability through StEMory and the Envisioned Role of Social Robots.

Wang, J., Alghowinem, S.,... & Breazeal, C. (2025).

Proceedings of ACM CSCW '28. Under review.

Keywords: Human-centered computing , Applied computing, Digital game, Group game, Social-emotional skill, Interpersonal closeness, Self-disclosure, Mental well-being, Social robots

Abstract: This study is part of a broader research project exploring the design of a social robot that facilitates a digital group game intervention aimed at enhancing participants’ social-emotional skills and mental well-being. At this stage, the research focuses on developing a digital visual-emotional-stimuli-based group game, StEMory (Stimulated Emotional Memory), bringing participants’ emotional safety to self-disclose, and fostering trust and interpersonal closeness in group settings. A mixed-method design has been implemented to measure the significance of game effects and explore users’ underlying psychological key for group interpersonal connectedness, as well as their views on the potential role a social robot could play in facilitating the game. A key design feature of the game is the adoption of the emotion-memory cognitive model while implementing complex emotional images for deeper cognitive processing. Within a theoretical framework built upon psychological theories, the study findings indicate a significant increase in both interpersonal closeness and individual-level willingness to engage in self-disclosure. This study also establishes a foundation for future CSCW research by developing a design guide for integrating a social robot into the group social-emotional intervention.

Designing Social Robots to Facilitate Social-Emotional Skills in Interactive Games.

Wang, J., Alghowinem, S.,... & Breazeal, C. (2025).

ICA '75. Extended abstract under review.

Keywords: Social Robot, Social-emotional skill, CASA Model, Human-Computer Interaction, Game Design, Group Game

Abstract: This study explores the integration of an embodied social robot within a hybrid in-person and digital group game designed to enhance social-emotional skills and foster group bonding among participants. Building upon existing theoretical frameworks, we examine the potential roles for embodied social robots—therapeutic, educational, and facilitative—each aimed at strengthening social-emotional connections within a structured, interactive game environment. A mixed-method design involving pre- and post-surveys and Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)--based assessments will evaluate how each robot role influences user experience, perceived bonding, and emotional engagement. The study result will contribute to the theoretical extension of Computers as Social Actors (CASA) and empirical applications of embodied social robot design in a group setting.

Self-Disclosure in Virtual Agents: How Self-Disclosure in Digital Companions Touches Our Emotional Engagement through Content and Modality.

Wang, J., Sun, Y. (2025).

ICA '75. Manuscript under review.

Keywords: Virtual Agents, Self-Disclosure, Emotional Engagement, Voice Modality, Human-Computer Interaction, CASA Model

Abstract: This experimental study investigates how self-disclosure by virtual agents sparks emotional engagement. Specifically, the extent of self-disclosure (high or low) and the modality (text-only or voice-inclusive) are manipulated to examine the effectiveness under different conditions. Emotional engagement was measured biometrically, including heart rate and skin conductance. Preliminary analysis reveals that a virtual agent with personal-related text disclosure and voice gains the most emotional engagement from users, though it also has the highest tendency to bring the cognitive load. While the current data supports informative analysis, data collection is still in progress to strengthen the statistical significance of identified trends. Further analysis that will allow more probing for insights will be conducted prior to the 2025 ICA conference. This research is expected to provide insights into the design and adoption of virtual agents in tasks requiring emotional connection.
