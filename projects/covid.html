COVID-19 War Narrative Project

Observing Public Anger and Criticism on Social Media at the Onset of the Pandemic

At the start of the pandemic, there was widespread public anger on Chinese social media, with people expressing frustration over the government’s handling of the crisis. Many felt that the concealment of information led to unnecessary loss of life, and there was strong criticism of what people saw as inadequate government responses and measures.

Identifying the “COVID-19 War” Narrative as a Reconciliation Tool

Over time, the initial anger on social media began to soften as a new narrative emerged—the “COVID-19 war” narrative. This narrative became a powerful tool for reconciling public sorrow and anger, transforming the crisis into a story of national resilience and collective effort against a common enemy.

Master’s Thesis Research on Collective Psychology and Wartime Framing

I explored this topic in my master’s thesis at NYU, focusing on how a wartime narrative could help reconcile public emotions through the lens of collective psychology. My research examined how collective grief and frustration could be reframed into a sense of shared sacrifice and duty, offering psychological closure and purpose.

Data Collection and Analysis of Chinese Social Media Content on Weibo

For my research, I gathered data from official Chinese social media accounts on Weibo. Using topic modeling, I was able to identify specific themes that framed the virus as an “enemy” and positioned the public health management team as “soldiers.”

Findings: Reframing Sorrow and Loss as Necessary Sacrifice for National Resilience

My findings revealed that the wartime narrative shifted the perception of personal loss into a necessary sacrifice for the nation’s greater good. This narrative allowed individual sorrow to be integrated into a broader story of resilience, aligning personal experiences with the collective psychology of national endurance.
