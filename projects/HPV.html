HPV Vaccination Research

Application of the Health Belief Model (HBM)

Used the HBM framework to analyze HPV vaccine discourse on social media, focusing on core HBM constructs: perceived susceptibility, severity, benefits, barriers, and self-efficacy.

By mapping these constructs to real-world Twitter narratives, assessed how individuals perceive the HPV vaccine’s importance, benefits, and barriers, identifying critical factors in vaccine hesitancy and acceptance.

Developing a GPT-Driven Data Identification Tool

Created the GPT-HBM Program, a Python-based tool leveraging the OpenAI API and GPT-4 model, to automate the classification of social media posts according to HBM variables. This tool allowed analysis of large datasets with greater speed and accuracy, reducing human labor typically required for data coding.

Experimenting with Prompt Engineering for Model Accuracy

Applied an experimental approach to prompt engineering, refining the model’s handling of nuanced HBM variables by tailoring prompts based on specific health belief constructs. This process included iterative testing, reviewing, and adjusting prompts to address model performance.

Ensured high Krippendorff’s alpha scores (0.87 - 0.96) in alignment with human coders, enhancing the model’s capacity for interpreting complex constructs like perceived susceptibility and self-efficacy.

Time Series Analysis of HPV Vaccine Discourse

Applied time series analysis to study the influence of public health events, such as Cervical Cancer Awareness Month, on behavioral tendencies related to HPV vaccination. Findings indicated limited impact from such campaigns, as behavior patterns on social media didn’t show significant shifts towards increased vaccine uptake during these periods.

Descriptive and Regression Analysis of HBM Constructs

Conducted descriptive analysis and regression modeling to assess the prevalence and relationships between HBM variables. Highlighted perceived severity and benefits as dominant themes in the discourse, while perceived susceptibility and self-efficacy were less frequently mentioned, pointing to areas where public education may need strengthening.
